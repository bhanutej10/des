02
Context & Situation

The Transition Shock

Indian colleges see a sharp spike in loneliness in semesters 1–2. Students arrive from tight-knit hometown communities and face impersonal urban institutions.

Post-COVID cohort

Social Hierarchies Form Fast

Regional language groups, JEE rank clusters, and coaching institute alumni networks solidify quickly — leaving many permanently on the outside.

Metro colleges

The Silence Culture

Family expectations create performance pressure. "Log kya kahenge" (what will people say) culture silences vulnerability and discourages help-seeking.

Tier-1 institutions

Digital Trap: Instagram creates a highlight reel — students see peers "having fun" and feel worse about their own isolation.

04
Pain Points & Needs

Emotional

No safe space to express homesickness, anxiety, or self-doubt without being judged or seen as "weak." Mental health support is scarce and stigmatised.

Social

Friend groups solidify in week 1. Latecomers or shy students find it nearly impossible to break in later, creating permanent social exclusion.

Structural

Packed academic schedules leave no buffer time for organic socialising. Elective or club activities are under-promoted and hard to discover.

Root Causes (5 Whys)
Lack of deep, meaningful connectionsSurface-level friendships with no emotional intimacy or trust.
Poor communication & emotional expressionTeens are not taught to open up or show vulnerability in a healthy way.
Comparison & social pressureSocial media highlight reels fuel persistent feelings of being "less than."
Personal factorsLow self-worth, overthinking, perfectionism, and past emotional wounds compound isolation.
Environmental & lifestyle factorsAcademic pressure, excessive screen time, and a lack of shared offline activities.
Transitions & uncertaintyNew city, new campus, unclear identity, and fear of the future leave students adrift.

Prototype: Peer Buddy System

Each newcomer is assigned a trained peer buddy before Day 1 of college — a simple, human-centred solution to week-1 social exclusion.

Measurement: UCLA Loneliness Scale (short form) compared to baseline at week 5. Success metric: reduced loneliness score + positive buddy feedback.

Scale-up: If successful, roll out college-wide every semester. Buddy pairs receive recognition certificates + priority club access.

W0

Registration

Volunteers from 2nd year+ sign up as peer buddies. Matched with one newcomer each based on department & shared interests.

D1

First Contact

Buddy reaches out via WhatsApp before orientation. Offers to meet, show around campus, sit together at events.

W1–4

Weekly Check-ins

Buddy checks in at least once a week — a text, a chai, or a walk. No formal agenda, just presence and warmth.

W5

Measure Impact

Newcomer fills a 5-question wellbeing form. "Did you feel less alone? Did your buddy help?" UCLA scale vs baseline.

→

Scale

If successful: Roll out college-wide every semester. Buddy pairs get a recognition certificate and priority access to campus clubs.

Research & Literature Review

This project draws on peer-reviewed research from the International Journal of Indian Psychology and other sources to build an evidence-backed understanding of loneliness in Indian youth.

IJIP · 2014 · Vol 1, Issue 4

Adjustment, Emotional Control & Perceived Loneliness Among Adolescents

Anu Agarwal · Banasthali University, Rajasthan

Studied 120 adolescents (ages 14–17) comparing day scholars vs hostellers on adjustment, emotional control, and loneliness. Key finding: hostellers experience significantly more loneliness than day scholars. Type of schooling significantly affected social adjustment and perceived loneliness.

Key finding: Hostellers scored higher on the UCLA Loneliness Scale (mean 50.73) vs day scholars (48.41). The interaction effect of type of schooling and gender on perceived loneliness was also significant (F=6.71, p<.05).

UCLA Loneliness Scale Hostel vs Day-scholar 2×2 ANOVA n=120 adolescents Social adjustment
IJIP · 2020 · Vol 8, Issue 2

A Review of Loneliness in Indian Youth

Tapan K. Pandit · Sage University, Indore

A literature review identifying key factors driving loneliness in Indian youth. Migration, mobile/internet addiction, low self-esteem, body shaming, personality traits, and broken social structures are primary contributors. Notes that India's traditional joint-family buffer has collapsed.

Cited statistic: A Chandigarh study found 62% of adolescents reported feelings of loneliness. WHO noted 1 in 4 children aged 13–15 in India faces loneliness. Urban youth aged 20–30 increasingly report feeling friendless and isolated.
